FIRST IMPLEMENTATION (four pages):
# Introduction to Trigonometry

The goal of the whole sequence: never hand the student a ratio to memorize — every value in the closing table is watched being built from a triangle the student is holding.

## Part One — Naming the Ratios (trig)
One right triangle ABC with the right angle at C and a knob on B that reshapes it freely.
With respect to the focused acute angle, the opposite leg, adjacent leg, and hypotenuse each wear their own color, and the words "opposite", "adjacent", "hypotenuse" ride the sides themselves.
A panel builds all six ratios as colored fractions with live lengths and live values: sin = opp/hyp, cos = adj/hyp, tan = opp/adj, and beneath them their three flips csc, sec, cot, shown literally as the same fractions upside-down.
Click the other acute angle to move the focus: the opposite and adjacent colors trade places on the same triangle, and the panel shows sin A = cos B with equal live numbers — the cofunction identity sin A = cos(90° − A) embodied.
A footer computes sin²A + cos²A = (a² + b²)/c² live, always 1.00 — the Pythagorean Theorem wearing its trigonometric clothes.

## Part Two — Folding the Square (fortyfive)
A square of side a with a resize knob on one corner and a fold knob on the opposite corner.
Dragging the fold knob folds the square over its diagonal; the two halves land exactly on each other — congruent — so the fold bisects the corner right angles, and 45° arcs appear at both ends of the crease.
What remains is the 45-45-90 triangle: legs a and a, and the Pythagorean Theorem computes the hypotenuse √(a² + a²) = a√2 with live numbers.
The panel then builds each of the six ratios from those sides, showing the a's cancel: sin 45° = a/(a√2) = 1/√2 = √2/2 ≈ 0.707.
Resizing the square changes every decimal on the sides while every ratio holds still.

## Part Three — Folding the Equilateral (thirtysixty)
An equilateral triangle of side 2a, all three 60° angles marked, with a resize knob and a fold knob.
Dragging the fold knob folds the left half over the altitude; the halves match exactly — congruent by ASA — which is why the foot is the midpoint (the base splits into a + a) and the apex angle splits into 30° + 30°.
What remains is the 30-60-90 triangle: hypotenuse 2a, short leg a, and the Pythagorean Theorem computes the long leg √((2a)² − a²) = a√3 live, giving the ratio 1 : √3 : 2.
The panel derives both columns — every ratio of 30° and of 60° — with the cancellations shown (a/2a = 1/2), plus the callout that the hypotenuse is twice the leg opposite 30°.

## Part Four — The Whole Table (table)
A right triangle whose hypotenuse is pinned at length 1, so the opposite leg literally is sin θ and the adjacent leg literally is cos θ.
A protractor dial at A drives θ from 0° to 90°, snapping at 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, and 90°; at each snap the sides trade their decimals for the exact values just derived, and the caption names which special triangle you are holding.
At θ = 0° the triangle degenerates — B and C become the same point, so sin 0° = 0 and cos 0° = 1; at θ = 90° the adjacent leg vanishes and tan 90° is a division by zero: undefined.
The right side of the screen holds the full six-function table for 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, 90° in exact form, with the column nearest the dial highlighted and a live decimal column when between snaps.
The footer notes sin²θ + cos²θ = 1 with live numbers: on this unit-hypotenuse triangle the identity IS the Pythagorean Theorem.
