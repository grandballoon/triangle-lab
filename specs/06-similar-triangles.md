FIRST IMPLEMENTATION:
# Similar Triangles
One acute angle θ sits at A, between a horizontal base ray and a tilted hypotenuse ray, with a dial knob on its arc to change θ.
Dropping a perpendicular from any point of the tilted ray to the base ray makes a right triangle, and every such triangle shares the angles 90°, θ, and 90° − θ — that is AA similarity, the parallel vertical legs making it visible.
Two sample triangles are drawn at fixed spots on the ray, and a third belongs to the user: a knob slides along the hypotenuse ray to grow and shrink it continuously.
A panel shows a table: for each of the three triangles, its opposite leg, adjacent leg, and hypotenuse, followed by the three ratios opp/hyp, adj/hyp, and opp/adj.
Sliding the size knob changes every side length in the user's row while the three ratio columns stay identical down all three rows — the ratios refuse to move.
Turning the angle dial changes all three ratio columns together.
The punchline, stated in the panel: the ratio of two sides of a right triangle depends only on the angle, so each ratio is a function of θ alone — a function worth naming, which is the next lesson.
The dial snaps gently at 30°, 45°, and 60°, foreshadowing the angles the next lessons will derive exactly.
