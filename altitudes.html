Parts of a Triangle

Altitudes

What an altitude is

An altitude is a triangle's height, measured from one vertex: the perpendicular segment from that vertex to the line containing the opposite side. Its length is exactly the distance from the vertex to that line — and whenever we say the distance from a point to a line, we mean the length of the perpendicular dropped from the point to the line, because the perpendicular is the shortest of all segments joining them.

The phrase "line containing" is the part that trips people up, and it is the whole reason the obtuse triangle in the demo feels different. The foot of the perpendicular does not have to land on the side itself. In an obtuse triangle, two of the three altitudes miss their sides entirely: you must extend the side (the dashed line in the demo, like extending BC to D in the classic figure) before the perpendicular has anywhere to land. The altitude is still a perfectly good height — the side just isn't wide enough to catch it.

Notation, and why altitudes matter

The three altitudes are written ha, hb, and hc, where ha is the altitude from vertex A — which makes it perpendicular to side a (the side opposite A). Altitudes are the "height" in the area formula: any side can serve as the base, so one triangle has three base–height pairs, and ½ a ha = ½ b hb = ½ c hc all give the same area.

The orthocenter

The three altitude lines always pass through a single point, the orthocenter H. Where it lands depends on the triangle:

  • Acute: all three feet land on their sides, and H is inside the triangle.
  • Right: the two legs are perpendicular to each other, so each leg is the altitude from the vertex it starts at. Both legs meet at the right-angle vertex, so the answer is: H is the right-angle vertex itself.
  • Obtuse: two feet fall on extensions, and H is outside the triangle — the altitude segments never meet, only the lines through them do. And the flatter the triangle, the farther away H flies (scroll down in the demo to see).
  • Equilateral: every altitude is also a median, an angle bisector, and a perpendicular bisector — so H, the centroid G, the incenter, and the circumcenter O are all the same point.
  • Isosceles: the altitude from the apex is the axis of symmetry, and H always lies on it.

Compare with the circumcenter O from the perpendicular-bisector section: it is also inside an acute triangle, outside an obtuse one, and lands somewhere special on a right one. The exercises below pin down exactly where.

Example 11-1

Show that the circumcenter of a right triangle is the midpoint of its hypotenuse.

Proof. Draw the circumcircle of right triangle ABC, with the right angle at C. Since ∠C is an inscribed angle, the arc it cuts off satisfies arc AB = 2∠C = 180°. An arc of 180° is a semicircle, so the chord AB is a diameter of the circle. The center O is the midpoint of any diameter, so O is the midpoint of the hypotenuse AB. ∎

Exercise 11-1

Show that the circumradius of a right triangle is equal to half the hypotenuse.

Proof. By Example 11-1, the hypotenuse AB is a diameter of the circumcircle. A radius is half a diameter, so R = AB/2. ∎

Exercise 11-2

Show that the median to the hypotenuse of a right triangle is equal to half the hypotenuse.

Proof. The median to the hypotenuse runs from C to the midpoint M of AB. By Example 11-1, M is the circumcenter, and a circumcenter is at distance R from every vertex — including C. So the median has length MC = R = AB/2 by Exercise 11-1. ∎

Exercise 11-3

Show that if a median of a triangle is one-half the side to which it is drawn, then the triangle must be right.

Proof. Let the median run from C to the midpoint M of AB, with CM = AB/2. Since M is the midpoint, MA = MB = AB/2 = MC.

Triangle AMC is isosceles (MA = MC), so ∠MAC = ∠MCA = x. Triangle BMC is isosceles (MB = MC), so ∠MBC = ∠MCB = y. Adding the angles of triangle ABC:

x + y + (x + y) = 180°  ⟹  x + y = 90°.

But ∠ACB = x + y, so ∠C = 90° and the triangle is right. ∎

Another view: MA = MB = MC means the circle centered at M with radius AB/2 passes through all three vertices, with AB as a diameter — so ∠C is inscribed in a semicircle. This is the same circle the Angles lesson used: the apex snapped to 90° exactly when it crossed the circle whose diameter was the base.
